## Brief

FRC stands for FIRST Robotics Competition. It is a contest where high school teams build a robot to play a game. The game is new every year. Your team gets about six weeks to design, build, and program the robot. Then you take it to competitions and play with and against other teams.

Here is what a match looks like. At a competition, robots play short games called matches. Each match is about two and a half minutes long. Three teams join up as an alliance and play against another alliance of three teams. You score points by doing tasks in the game, like grabbing game pieces and putting them in the right spot. The first few seconds are called autonomous, where the robot drives itself using code. After that, the drivers take over.

The season has a rhythm. It starts with Kickoff, the day the new game is revealed. Then comes build season, about six weeks of designing and building. After that, you compete at events. Teams that do well can move on to bigger championships.

A team is much more than its drivers. People design parts in CAD, build and machine those parts, write code, plan strategy, scout other teams, and keep the notebook. There is a job for every kind of person, and the best teams need all of them.

This track takes you from brand-new member to real contributor, one unit at a time. Every unit has four steps. First you read the Brief to learn the topic. Then you practice with Drills, which work like a game, until you are ready. Then you take a Quiz to prove you learned it, and you need 90 percent to pass. Last, you do an Apply task to use the skill for real. You move at your own speed, and you earn ranks as you go.

You do not need any experience to start. Everyone here began as a beginner. Work through the units, ask for help when you get stuck, and you will be building real robot parts before you know it.

## Brief

Every FRC year follows the same rhythm. If you know the rhythm, you always know what the team should be working on.

It starts with Kickoff. This is the day the new game is revealed. The whole team watches the reveal, then reads the game manual to learn the rules and how to score points. Before you build anything, you have to understand the game.

Next is build season. This is about six weeks long, and it is the busy part. The team picks a strategy, designs the robot, builds and wires it, writes the code, and tests everything. You will not get it perfect the first time. You build, test, find problems, and fix them, over and over.

Then come competitions. At an event you first play qualification matches, and these matches set your ranking. After qualifications, the top-ranked teams pick partners in alliance selection. Then the best alliances play the playoffs, which are elimination rounds, to decide the winner.

Teams that do well move on to bigger events and even a Championship. After the season ends there is the off-season, a time to learn new skills, train new members, and get ready for next year.

The season is a sprint, and everyone has a part to play. Plan your time and keep the whole rhythm in mind.

## Brief

Safety comes first, every single time. A robot is never worth an injury, and the shop has tools that can hurt you if you are careless. The good news is that staying safe is mostly a set of simple habits.

Wear safety glasses any time tools are running, even if you are just watching nearby. Your eyes cannot be replaced.

Keep clear of moving parts. Tie back long hair, take off loose sleeves and jewelry, and never reach into a machine that is spinning. Loose things get caught, and that is how people get hurt.

Never use a tool or machine you have not been trained on. If you are not sure how something works, stop and ask a mentor. There is no shame in asking, but there is real danger in guessing.

Take care of the space and each other. Clean up messes so no one trips, report any injury no matter how small, and tell a mentor if a tool is broken. On the most dangerous machines, do not work alone.

Respect the machines and follow these habits, and the shop is a safe place to do great work.

## Brief

Engineering is really just a smart way to solve a problem. It does not matter if the problem is a robot arm, a piece of code, or a game plan. The same process works for all of them, and it is a loop, not a straight line.

First, understand the problem. Say clearly what you are trying to solve and what the limits are. If you skip this, you can build the wrong thing perfectly.

Next, come up with ideas. Think of a few different ways to solve it, not just the first one. More ideas means a better chance of finding a good one.

Then pick an idea and try it. Build a quick, rough version to see if it works. It does not need to be pretty. It needs to teach you something.

Now test it. See what works and what does not, and be honest about the results. Failing a test is not bad, it is how you learn what to fix.

Finally, improve it and go again. Use what you learned to make the next version better, then repeat the loop until it is good enough. The first version is never the last, and that is normal.

Later, the Mechanical Design units show how this same loop works when the thing you are designing is a real robot part.

## Brief

The engineering notebook is the team's memory. It is the written record of what you did, why you did it, and what you learned. Without it, the team forgets its own good ideas and repeats its own mistakes.

Why does it matter so much? It lets a new member pick up where someone left off. It helps the team make smart choices by looking back at what happened before. And at competitions, judges read it to understand your team's work.

What do you write? Keep it simple. Write what you did, why you chose it, what happened, and what you learned. Add the date and your name. A good test is this: could someone else continue your work using only what you wrote down?

Write down your failures too. When something breaks or an idea does not work, that is worth recording, because the whole team learns from it. Hiding a failure just means someone repeats it later.

Everyone contributes. If you worked on the robot today, you have something to add. Keep your entries clear and honest, and the notebook becomes one of the team's most powerful tools.
